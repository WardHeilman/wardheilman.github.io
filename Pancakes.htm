Pancakes

 

Many years ago I was living in a communal apartment with my girlfriend, my friend, Steve, and his girlfriend, Jane, Jane’s sister, Susan, Susan’s daughter, Adrienne, and Susan’s current boyfriend, Eric. Steve, Jane, my girlfriend, and I were all somewhat college educated with limited experience in the wider world. Susan only dated African American men preferably those who had been (or even still were) in prison. Eric looked down on us privileged white kids who, he thought, probably couldn’t survive 10 minutes in the Chicago neighborhood where he grew up. He viewed himself as a practical person who “got things done” while we futzed around, over-analyzed every move and talked topics to death.

The apartment was on the second story of an old building in downtown Harrisburg. It was ostensibly in Steve and Jane’s name, so they had the big bedroom. The rest of us slept where we could: couches, the floor or, in good weather, on the outside porch overlooking Second Street.  While we waited for the coming revolution, some of us waitressed, some parked cars at the fancy hotel downtown, some were counselors at public agencies, some worked at the Y. We shared what little money we had. And we prepared and ate collectively.

Adrienne was one of those wise children, smarter, more thoughtful, and abnormally compassionate. She was a wonder. We all knew she was meant for something extraordinary.  Except that she got sick and then got really sick. She had to take steroids and gained a lot of weight. But she never complained: she thought first of others. She always was concerned about what the rest of us were feeling, even when she was so weak she couldn’t get out of bed.

When she died, everyone was devastated. There is no way to express how the world ended for Susan. We were inconsolable. We discovered that heartbroken was not just a word. It ended our small, odd community.

But before that, we were an unstable, happy group of people with just enough for all of what we were.

One morning months before Adrienne died, we decided that we were going to make a big breakfast. My girlfriend, Steve, Jane, and I all went into the kitchen trying to figure out what we could make. Our stock of supplies was usually fairly small and brittle; this morning it was pitiful. The four of us started to list the kinds of ingredients we might need to make various breakfasts and went through the options, carefully eliminating them as we realized we didn’t have one or another of the ingredients.  A staple in our household, scrambled eggs was out of the question, as we only had one egg. And this also excluded French toast and any of our variants based on whatever we could soak in eggy milk (or eggy water).  Two different cans of beans were examined and put aside; we were not that desperate, and besides, dinner was coming. Eventually we fell upon the idea of pancakes. How hard could it be to assemble pancakes of some sort. We picked, considered and put back a whole array of left over, left out and extraneous oddball foodstuffs. It seemed each time we discarded some item, someone would say something like “we don’t need old pickles, we need some flour and baking soda”, or “we don’t need that ancient tin of anchovies, we need some olive oil”.  Eric watched us with disdain for a very long time as we critiqued the items in our cupboard and analyzed all the refuse and leftovers in the refrigerator.  Finally, when it was said, for the tenth time,  “We need … but we don’t have any”,  Eric exploded, “We don’t need all that shit, WHAT WE NEED IS SOME PANCAKES!”

He proceeded to kick us all out of the kitchen. In short order he produced a stack of very edible pancakes and presented them on the table with a level of scorn and contempt that a king might reserve for his lowliest subject.

Ever since, when I hear some pompous presentation, some overblown description of how to do something, when I hear surplus, verbose phony declarations about some subject, or any mystical language which will never lead to a solution nor the creation of anything useful, when I hear too much talk about a goal like justice or equity without any plan or commitment, filled with vague, worthless action-empty phrases, I think to myself,

 “We don’t need high impact scaffolding; we don’t need more talk about justice; WHAT WE NEED IS SOME PANCAKES.”
